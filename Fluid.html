Comprehensive Hydration Guide

Oral Rehydration Solutions

WHO Standard Oral Rehydration Solution

The gold standard solution for treating dehydration from diarrhea

Ingredients:

Clean water 1 liter (boiled and cooled or bottled)
Sugar 6 level teaspoons (25g)
Salt ½ level teaspoon (2.9g)

Preparation:

Mix all ingredients until completely dissolved

Usage:

  • Infants: 50-100mL after each loose stool
  • Children: 100-200mL after each loose stool
  • Adults: As much as wanted, up to 2L/day
Reference: World Health Organization. (2005). The Treatment of Diarrhoea: A Manual for Physicians

Homemade Alternatives

Rice-Based ORS

Alternative when sugar is unavailable

Rice water 1 liter (from 50g rice cooked in 1L water)
Salt ½ teaspoon
Reference: Journal of Pediatric Gastroenterology and Nutrition. (2000). Rice-based oral rehydration solution for treating acute diarrhoea

Fruit Juice ORS

For improved palatability

Clean water 750mL
Orange juice 250mL
Salt ½ teaspoon

Condition-Specific Fluid Recommendations

Vomiting

Small Frequent Sips Protocol

Gradual rehydration for vomiting patients

  • Wait 10 minutes after vomiting before starting
  • Begin with 5mL (1 teaspoon) every 5 minutes
  • Double amount every hour if tolerated
  • Use standard ORS when possible
Reference: Journal of Pediatric Gastroenterology and Nutrition. (2014). Acute Gastroenteritis Guidelines

Excessive Sweating

Electrolyte Replacement Drink

Water 1 liter
Sugar 6 teaspoons
Salt ½ teaspoon
Orange juice ½ cup (for potassium)
Reference: Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise. (2007). ACSM Position Stand on Exercise and Fluid Replacement

Burns

Burn Rehydration Solution

Warning: Interim measure only - seek medical care immediately
Water 1 liter
Sugar 6 teaspoons
Salt ½ teaspoon
Baking soda ½ teaspoon (for bicarbonate)

Usage: 1L in first hour for adults, then as tolerated

Reference: Journal of Burn Care & Research. (2013). Oral Rehydration Therapy for Burn Shock

Fever

Fever Hydration Protocol

  • Increase fluids by 10-15% per 1°C above normal
  • Offer fluids every 15-30 minutes
  • Use lukewarm fluids (not ice-cold)
  • Include electrolytes if fever persists >24 hours
Reference: Pediatrics. (2011). Fever and Antipyretic Use in Children

Kidney Stones

General Stone Prevention

  • Water: 3L+ per day (goal >2.5L urine output)
  • Lemon water: 120mL lemon juice in 2L water daily
  • Avoid: Grapefruit juice, colas, excessive caffeine
Reference: Journal of Urology. (2014). Medical Management of Kidney Stones

Stone-Type Specific

  • Calcium oxalate: Citrus juices, moderate calcium intake
  • Uric acid: Alkaline fluids (citrus, vegetable juices)
  • Cystine: Very high fluid intake (>4L/day)

Age-Specific Guidelines

Infants (0-12 months)

Daily Requirements: 150-200mL/kg/day (0-6mo), 120-150mL/kg/day (6-12mo)

Special Notes:

  • Breast milk or formula meets all hydration needs
  • Avoid plain water in large amounts (risk of hyponatremia)
  • Double fluid intake during illness
Reference: American Academy of Pediatrics. (2019). Pediatric Nutrition Handbook

Elderly (65+ years)

Daily Requirements: 30mL/kg/day

Special Notes:

  • Reduced thirst sensation increases dehydration risk
  • Schedule fluid intake rather than relying on thirst
  • Monitor for confusion, dry mouth, sunken eyes
Reference: Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews. (2014). Water-loss dehydration and aging

Activity Level Guidelines

Sedentary

Recommendations: Men: 3.7L/day, Women: 2.7L/day (includes food moisture)

Monitoring: Urine color (pale yellow ideal)

Athletes

Hydration Strategy

  • Pre-exercise: 5-7mL/kg 4 hours before
  • During: 0.4-0.8L/hour based on sweat rate
  • Post: 1.5L per kg body weight lost
  • Electrolytes: 20-30mEq/L sodium for >1hr exercise
Reference: American College of Sports Medicine. (2007). Exercise and Fluid Replacement Position Stand

Outdoor Workers

Recommendations

  • Drink 250mL every 15-20 minutes in heat
  • Include electrolytes for shifts >4 hours
  • Weigh before/after shift to gauge fluid loss

Emergency Signs

Severe Dehydration

  • No urine for 8+ hours
  • Sunken eyes (in children)
  • Extreme lethargy or confusion
  • Rapid weak pulse
  • Cold extremities

Immediate Actions

  • Begin oral rehydration if conscious
  • Seek emergency medical care
  • Prepare for possible IV fluids
