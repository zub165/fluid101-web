Oral Rehydration Solutions Guide

Learn how to make and use oral rehydration solutions for various conditions to prevent and treat dehydration

Dehydration from Diarrhea

When is Rehydration Needed (Symptoms)

  • Frequent loose, watery stools (3 or more in a day) leading to fluid loss
  • Signs of dehydration: thirst and dry mouth; dark yellow urine or urinating less often
  • Sunken eyes, or in babies a sunken soft spot on the head (fontanelle)
  • Lethargy, irritability, or dizziness/lightheadedness due to low fluids
  • In infants: few or no tears when crying, and significantly fewer wet diapers

Homemade Rehydration Recipe

This simple Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) replaces lost water, sugars, and salts using common kitchen ingredients:

  1. Take 1 liter (about 4¼ cups) of safe drinking water (boiled and cooled, or bottled water)
  2. Add 6 teaspoons of sugar and ½ teaspoon of salt into the water
  3. Stir thoroughly until the sugar and salt are completely dissolved
  4. Option: For flavor and extra electrolytes, you may add ½ cup of 100% orange juice or mashed banana
Note: Measure ingredients carefully – too much salt or sugar can be harmful. Always use level measurements. Use clean water only. Do not add other ingredients beyond the recipe.

Usage Guidelines

  • Start giving the solution as soon as diarrhea begins – do not wait for severe dehydration
  • Children under 2 years: Give about 50–100 mL (¼ to ½ cup) of ORS after each loose stool
  • Children 2 to 9 years: Give ~100–200 mL (½ to 1 cup) after each loose stool
  • Older children and adults: Drink as much as wanted, aiming for up to ~2 liters per day
  • Continue regular feeding as tolerated: infants should continue breast milk or their normal formula alongside ORS

When to Seek Medical Attention

  • Signs of severe dehydration: extreme lethargy, inability to drink, no urination for 8+ hours
  • If diarrhea is bloody or black, or accompanied by persistent high fever or severe abdominal pain
  • If the person is showing signs of shock (weak rapid pulse, confusion, fainting)
  • In infants under 6 months, any dehydration can become serious quickly

Dehydration from Vomiting

When is Rehydration Needed (Symptoms)

  • Repeated bouts of vomiting especially if the person cannot keep liquids down for several hours
  • Dry mouth and tongue, cracked lips, or absence of saliva
  • Thirst and irritability
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness, especially when standing up
  • In babies and young children: sunken eyes or sunken fontanelle, no tears when crying

Homemade Rehydration Recipe

Use the same sugar-salt solution (ORS) recipe to rehydrate after vomiting episodes:

  1. Prepare 1 liter of clean water and mix in 6 tsp sugar + ½ tsp salt, stirred until dissolved
  2. Ensure the solution is at room temperature
  3. Optionally, you can freeze some ORS in an ice cube tray or as popsicles
Note: If even tiny sips of ORS trigger vomiting, try other mild fluids temporarily like clear diluted apple juice or flat ginger ale mixed with water.

Usage Guidelines

  • Start slow: Wait 5–10 minutes after vomiting, then begin with small amounts
  • Young children (under ~5 years): Use a spoon or medicine syringe to give ~5 mL of fluid at a time
  • Older children and teens: Start with small sips (~1–2 tablespoons) every 5-10 minutes
  • Adults: Take small sips or ice chips frequently
  • Monitor urine output - should urinate at least a few times per day

When to Seek Medical Attention

  • If every attempt to drink results in immediate vomiting for more than 4–6 hours
  • Watch for signs of worsening dehydration: no urine for 8 hours, worsening lethargy or confusion
  • If vomit is bloody, dark green, or looks like "coffee grounds"
  • If accompanied by high fever (above 39°C / 102°F), stiff neck, or severe headache

Emergency Warning Signs

Seek immediate medical attention if you observe:

  • No urine for 8+ hours
  • Extreme lethargy or confusion
  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Sunken eyes (in children)
  • Fainting or loss of consciousness
Call Emergency Services
